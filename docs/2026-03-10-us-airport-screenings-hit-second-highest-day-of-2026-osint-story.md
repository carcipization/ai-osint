## Story
U.S. checkpoint traffic climbed to one of its strongest daily readings of the year at the start of March, adding evidence that the spring travel rush is moving from forecast into observed volume.

Transportation Security Administration (TSA) data show 2,781,523 travelers screened on Sunday, March 8. That is the second-highest daily count in the posted 2026 series, trailing only Jan. 4 (2,817,509), which came during the New Year return window.

The move was not just a one-day jump. The trailing seven-day average ending March 8 reached 2,415,608 screenings, up 3.33% from 2,337,661 in the prior seven-day window. On a same-weekday check, March 8 ran 11.55% above the average of the previous four Sundays.

Those readings broadly align with an Airlines for America (A4A) forecast released Feb. 24 projecting about 2.8 million U.S. airline passengers per day across March and April. A4A is an industry trade group, not a neutral regulator, but the TSA administrative counts now sit close to that projected pace.

Why it matters: for airport operators, airlines and security staffing planners, the data point to a higher-throughput operating environment now rather than later in spring. If this level persists through mid-March, schedule resilience and checkpoint staffing become near-term execution issues, not just peak-holiday concerns.

## Appendix: Method
- Pulled TSA checkpoint daily totals from the public TSA passenger-volume page.
- Recomputed rolling and comparative statistics directly from posted values:
  - trailing 7-day average ending 2026-03-08,
  - previous 7-day average,
  - latest Sunday versus average of the prior four Sundays.
- Cross-checked directional consistency against A4A’s published Mar-Apr demand forecast.

## Appendix: Limitations
- TSA’s public table is a rolling window; this check does not include a full multi-year seasonality model.
- A4A forecast figures are industry-provided and may carry advocacy bias.
- Calendar effects (school break timing, event clustering) can temporarily lift traffic without implying a durable trend.
