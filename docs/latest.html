ECDC count rises to 11 in Andes-cruise cluster while CDC risk posture remains extremely low

Dateline: 2026-05-13 08:25 UTC

A fresh ECDC update reports the Andes-virus cruise cluster at 11 total cases (9 confirmed, 2 probable) after disembarkation in Tenerife, while CDC’s U.S. posture still says no confirmed U.S. outbreak-linked cases and an extremely low risk to the public.

What changed

The latest cross-agency delta is in case accounting and operational status: ECDC now reports 11 total linked cases and completed disembarkation/repatriation (11 May), while CDC keeps its risk framing unchanged.

Evidence

  1. ECDC update (12 May 2026) (retrieved 2026-05-13 08:25 UTC): reports 11 total cases (9 confirmed, 2 probable), completed disembarkation/repatriation, and very low risk to EU/EEA general population.
  2. CDC Andes-virus situation summary (retrieved 2026-05-13 08:25 UTC): states no confirmed U.S. cases tied to this outbreak and overall risk to the American public/travelers remains extremely low.
  3. WHO DON600 (retrieved 2026-05-13 08:25 UTC): last public WHO bulletin still anchors at 8 cases as of 8 May with low global risk.

Hypotheses and adjudication

  • H1 (supported): Case totals increased in European surveillance streams after WHO’s last public count cut-off.
  • H2 (supported): Higher linked-case count has not yet translated into broader population-risk escalation in CDC/ECDC language.
  • H3 (uncertain): WHO will shortly harmonize to the 11-case figure in a new DON update; plausible but not yet public.

Limitations

  • Inter-agency publication cadence is asynchronous; counts can differ by reporting cut-off.
  • Public reports do not expose full line-list reconciliation details.

Confidence

Medium-High that the cluster total has risen above WHO’s last published count and High that U.S./EU general-population risk language remains low.

Why it matters

For decision-makers, this is a “count up, risk posture flat” signal: incident tracking should update numerators and exposure follow-up, but current evidence does not justify broad policy escalation.
