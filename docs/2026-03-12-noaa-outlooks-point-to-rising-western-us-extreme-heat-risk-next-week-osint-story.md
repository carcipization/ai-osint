## Story
NOAA forecast products now show a stronger signal for unusually hot conditions across the western United States next week, including an explicit extreme-heat risk window for parts of Southern California and the Desert Southwest.

In its latest week-2 hazards outlook, NOAA’s Climate Prediction Center (CPC) flagged a **moderate risk of much above-normal temperatures** for parts of the Great Basin, Rockies, California and the Desert Southwest on March 19–20, plus a **slight risk of extreme heat** over parts of Southern California and southwestern Arizona in the same window. CPC’s discussion says ensemble tools indicate roughly **40–50% odds** that maximum temperatures exceed the local 98th percentile in part of the region, with uncalibrated guidance showing **10–30% odds** of topping 105°F in the Sonoran Desert corridor.

A separate NOAA Weather Prediction Center (WPC) medium-range discussion supports the same direction of travel, describing “widespread record breaking values possible” from California into the Southwest as strong upper-level ridging builds.

The practical signal is not that a record is guaranteed at any single site, but that the probability field has moved high enough for agencies to treat heat as an operational risk before the period begins. That can change near-term actions on cooling-center readiness, utility load planning and outdoor-work heat safety messaging.

What could overturn this conclusion: if upcoming CPC/WPC update cycles weaken ridge intensity or shorten duration, the extreme-heat footprint could contract and leave mainly above-normal, non-extreme warmth.

## Appendix: Limitations
- This is a forecast-window assessment, not a post-event verification.
- CPC percentile and threshold probabilities are model-based and can shift between cycles.
- Local realized temperatures depend on mesoscale effects not resolved in a high-level national outlook.
