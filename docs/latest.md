## Story
Brent crude fell another roughly 5% on Wednesday to around $98 by midday London time, according to Reuters, extending this week’s drop below $100 and offering short-run relief pressure for fuel buyers, freight operators and inflation-sensitive households.

The best-tested mechanism is a policy-risk reset rather than a clean supply recovery: Reuters reported that a U.S. 15-point proposal to Iran raised ceasefire expectations, but shipping through the Strait of Hormuz remains heavily disrupted and producers are still rerouting flows. The International Energy Agency (IEA) said in its March Oil Market Report that nearly 20 million barrels per day of crude and product exports are disrupted, with the coordinated emergency stock release acting as a buffer while normal transit remains impaired.

Fresh physical data points still argue for volatility, not normalization. Reuters reported Saudi crude exports from Yanbu rose to nearly 4 million barrels per day last week as cargoes were rerouted away from Hormuz, while separate Reuters reporting said Russia’s Baltic terminals at Primorsk and Ust-Luga suspended loadings after major drone attacks. For non-specialists, the decision implication is practical: treat current pump-price relief as fragile and keep contingency assumptions for renewed fuel-cost spikes if shipping security or parallel supply routes worsen.

What could overturn this: sustained safe transit through Hormuz plus stable alternative export routes would weaken the current high-volatility thesis and make this week’s price retreat more durable.
