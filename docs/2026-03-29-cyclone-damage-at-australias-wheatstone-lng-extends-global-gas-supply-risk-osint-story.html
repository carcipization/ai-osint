Cyclone damage at Australia’s Wheatstone LNG extends global gas supply risk

Human-readable HTML: HTML LLM-friendly Markdown: Markdown

Dateline: 2026-03-29 17:40 UTC

Initial lead: Reuters reporting on March 29 that repairs at Chevron’s Wheatstone LNG facility in Western Australia are likely to take “a number of weeks” after Tropical Cyclone Narelle damage (Reuters).

Story

A weather-driven disruption that began as a short outage risk in Australia has shifted into a multi-week LNG supply constraint, keeping gas-market stress elevated for import-dependent buyers.

Chevron said on Sunday that its two-train Wheatstone LNG project (8.9 million tonnes per year) is unlikely to return to full production for several weeks while storm damage is repaired. That is a meaningful escalation from earlier restart language focused on safety and inspections. At the same time, Chevron said Gorgon (15.9 mtpa) had returned to full rates, which partially offsets the shock but does not remove it.

The practical consequence is persistence, not just magnitude: markets and buyers now face a longer disruption window at Wheatstone while the Middle East supply corridor remains unstable. Reuters also reported that Woodside’s Karratha gas plant continued to face interruption on Sunday, adding further uncertainty around Australia’s export-normalization timeline.

For non-specialists, this matters because LNG disruptions feed into electricity and heating costs in gas-importing economies, then into broader household bills and industrial input costs. For operators, the signal is to plan for prolonged tightness rather than a rapid post-storm snapback.

What could overturn this: a faster-than-expected Wheatstone restoration timeline with confirmed sustained output, plus clear improvement in parallel disruptions (including Karratha continuity and broader Middle East shipping risk), would weaken the current elevated-risk read.
