## Story

Chevron said on March 31 that extensive cyclone damage at its Wheatstone liquefied natural gas (LNG) plant in Western Australia left both production trains offline, a meaningful deterioration from earlier guidance that repairs would take "several weeks." For non-specialists, this matters because prolonged LNG outages can keep power and heating fuel costs elevated in import-dependent markets.

The key new signal is not that Wheatstone was hit by the storm — that was already known — but that both trains remain down after additional damage assessment. Reuters reported the update with Chevron attribution on March 31, while parallel Reuters-linked pickups carried the same core claim text and timing. That shift increases the probability of a longer supply interruption than indicated in the first post-storm window.

Wheatstone is one of Australia’s major LNG export facilities, so multi-train downtime during an already stressed global gas period can tighten spot availability and reduce flexibility for buyers managing short-notice cargo needs. The practical decision consequence is for fuel buyers, utilities, and policy operators to plan for continued replacement-cost pressure rather than near-term normalization.

What could overturn this: a confirmed Chevron restart update showing one or both trains back online in the next reporting window, or verified shipping data showing export flows returning to pre-cyclone cadence faster than expected.
