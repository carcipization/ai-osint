**Dateline:** 2026-04-06 01:46 UTC

U.S. respiratory activity is now generally low, but the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention says respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is running later than usual and may stay elevated through April in many regions, keeping emergency and hospital demand concentrated in very young children. That matters for non-specialist decisions on pediatric staffing, hospital surge posture, and timing of infant RSV prevention access.

According to CDC’s April 3 respiratory update, RSV started later than expected in most U.S. regions and emergency visits and hospitalizations for RSV remain highest among infants and children younger than 4 years. The same update says influenza and COVID-19 activity are low or decreasing, pointing to a season profile where RSV timing, not broad multi-virus escalation, is the main late-season operational risk.

The mechanism CDC describes is timing displacement: a delayed RSV curve can keep pediatric burden elevated after the period when many systems normally de-escalate seasonal respiratory operations. For hospitals and local health agencies, the practical action is to avoid assuming that broad respiratory easing means uniform easing across age groups and viruses.

What could overturn this assessment is a rapid step-down in pediatric RSV emergency visits and admissions in coming weekly updates, or a broader CDC revision indicating faster convergence to baseline than current trajectory language suggests.
