## Story
Brent crude settled at $108.01 on Thursday, as a second supply shock from Russia’s western export system landed on top of still-disrupted shipping through the Strait of Hormuz.

Reuters reported that about 40% of Russia’s crude export capacity — roughly 2 million barrels per day by Reuters calculations — was halted after damage and disruptions across Primorsk, Ust-Luga, Novorossiysk, the Druzhba pipeline corridor, and Russia-linked tanker movements. In parallel, Reuters market coverage said investors were pricing prolonged conflict risk in the Middle East, with Brent settling at $108.01 as hopes for a quick de-escalation faded.

At the same time, Hormuz access remains selective rather than broadly normalized. Reuters reported Iran-linked messaging and political statements indicating some country-specific tanker passages are being allowed, but the broader lane is still treated as heavily constrained. AP’s shipping synthesis, citing Lloyd’s List Intelligence and Kpler, similarly indicates that traffic is still far below pre-war throughput even though some vessels continue to pass.

For non-specialists, the practical implication is that fuel-price planning should treat this as a compounded-supply-risk phase, not a stable return-to-normal. The decision impact is immediate for households, freight buyers, public procurement teams, and any operator using short-term fuel assumptions for budgeting.

What could overturn this: verified restoration of major Russian western export routes and sustained multi-day normalization of Hormuz transit volumes across non-aligned cargoes and flag states.
