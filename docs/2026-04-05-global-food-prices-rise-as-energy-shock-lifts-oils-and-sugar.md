# Global food prices rise again as energy shock lifts oils and sugar

**Dateline:** 2026-04-05 01:46 UTC

Global food prices rose for a second month in March, with the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) reporting that higher energy costs pushed vegetable oil and sugar prices higher, a shift that can quickly affect household grocery budgets and aid planning in import-dependent countries.

Initial lead: Reuters reported the March move and FAO warning on April 3 ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/markets/us/world-food-prices-extend-rise-march-united-nations-fao-says-2026-04-03/)). This report adds the primary FAO commodity-level breakdown and decision-focused implications for near-term food-cost risk.

FAO said its Food Price Index averaged 128.5 points in March, up 2.4% month on month and 1.0% year on year, while still about 19.8% below the March 2022 peak. The strongest monthly pressure came from vegetable oils (+5.1%), while cereals rose 1.5% and sugar also increased, according to FAO’s March release.

What could overturn this risk framing is a rapid easing in energy and fertilizer prices before planting and input-buying decisions lock in. FAO’s own caveat is that prolonged conflict-driven input costs could reduce planting intensity and lower future yields, while a shorter shock could leave global cereal availability sufficient to cap broader price spillovers.

For non-specialists, the decision consequence is practical: households and food-dependent businesses should treat staple and cooking-oil costs as elevated-risk categories into the next planting cycle, while agencies and import planners should stress-test procurement timing and buffer stocks if fertilizer and shipping costs stay high.

## Appendix: Limitations

- Core measurement is a global traded-commodity basket, not a direct measure of local retail food inflation in any single country.
- FAO monthly values can be revised; local pass-through can be delayed or muted by subsidies, currency effects, and trade policy.
- Current mechanism relies heavily on FAO’s interpretation of conflict-linked energy/input pressure; independent confirmation of planting-response effects will require later acreage and yield data.
