Early-March U.S. airport screenings ran above late-February baseline as spring demand firmed

Human-readable HTML: HTML LLM-friendly Markdown: Markdown

Dateline: 2026-03-10 01:00 UTC

Story

U.S. airport checkpoint traffic stepped up in early March, with daily screening volumes running clearly above the pace seen in the prior late-February stretch.

Transportation Security Administration (TSA) checkpoint data show an average of 2,449,583 travelers screened per day from March 1-8, compared with 2,304,562 per day from Feb. 21-28. That is a 6.29% increase across adjacent eight-day windows.

The gain was not confined to a single outlier day. TSA’s trailing seven-day average ending March 8 rose to 2,415,608 screenings, up 3.33% from 2,337,661 in the previous seven-day period. March 8 itself posted 2,781,523 screenings, the second-highest single-day count in the posted 2026 series.

The direction of travel also lines up with an Airlines for America (A4A) spring forecast published Feb. 24 projecting about 2.8 million passengers per day across March and April. A4A is an airline trade group, not a regulator, but TSA’s administrative counts are now moving in that range on peak days.

Why it matters: airports, airlines and security operators are shifting from winter-level assumptions to spring-throughput operations now. If this early-March step-up persists, staffing and queue-management plans need to be calibrated to higher daily loads before major holiday peaks arrive.

Appendix: Method

  • Pulled TSA checkpoint traveler totals from TSA’s public passenger-volume page.
  • Recomputed two adjacent eight-day windows (2026-02-21 to 2026-02-28 vs 2026-03-01 to 2026-03-08) and calculated the percent change.
  • Recomputed trailing seven-day average ending 2026-03-08 and compared it with the prior seven-day window.
  • Used A4A’s published Mar-Apr projection only as directional context, not as a primary measurement source.

Appendix: Limitations

  • TSA publishes a rolling table; this analysis uses the currently posted 2026 window, not a full multi-year seasonal model.
  • A4A figures are trade-group projections and may reflect advocacy incentives.
  • Adjacent-window comparisons can still be influenced by school-break timing and event clustering.

Appendix: Confidence

Confidence: Medium

Rationale: Confidence is high in TSA’s observed administrative counts and arithmetic recomputation. Confidence is moderate on persistence because the conclusion is based on short windows during a seasonally volatile period.
