Hormuz selective-transit signals emerge, but open-passage timeline remains unclear

Initial lead: Reuters report that the White House is tracking how to get oil tankers through the Strait of Hormuz, while giving no timeline for free passage (https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/us-tracking-closely-how-get-oil-tankers-through-strait-hormuz-white-house-says-2026-03-25/).

Story

New signals suggest the Strait of Hormuz is moving from near-total disruption toward selective transit, but not to normal flow, a shift that matters for fuel buyers, shippers and governments planning for energy-cost risk over the next several weeks.

Reuters reported on Wednesday that the White House said the U.S. is tracking “very closely” how to get oil tankers through Hormuz, while saying there is no specific timeline for unrestricted movement. In parallel, Reuters reported on Tuesday that Iran told the United Nations Security Council and the International Maritime Organization that “non-hostile” vessels may transit if they coordinate with Iranian authorities.

Independent shipping reporting from AP, citing Lloyd’s List Intelligence and Kpler, indicates some vessels are crossing even as overall traffic remains far below pre-war norms and routing appears conditional. AP said around 89 ships crossed between March 1 and 15 versus roughly 100–135 daily passages before the war, and that Iran has still exported substantial oil volumes during the same window. The practical decision implication is that households, freight operators and procurement teams should treat lower spot prices as fragile until passage is both broader and predictable, not merely possible for a limited set of vessels.

What could overturn this: sustained multi-day evidence of broad, non-conditional transit across major flag states and cargo classes would weaken the “selective corridor” interpretation.

Appendix: Limitations

  • AP shipping and export figures are mediated by third-party commercial/industry datasets rather than a single public registry export.
  • Passage counts and traffic conditions can shift quickly with military and diplomatic developments.
  • Some cited figures cover partial windows (e.g., March 1–15) and may not represent end-of-month conditions.
