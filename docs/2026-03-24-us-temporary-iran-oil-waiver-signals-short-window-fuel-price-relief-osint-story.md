## Story
The U.S. Treasury’s Office of Foreign Assets Control said it issued Iran-related General License U authorizing delivery and sale of Iranian-origin crude oil and petroleum products loaded on vessels as of March 20, 2026, a policy shift that could ease near-term fuel-price pressure for households and freight buyers if physical flows materialize. OFAC described the action in a March 20 recent-actions notice.

Reuters reported the move as a 30-day sanctions waiver intended to add near-term supply to global markets during a high-volatility phase of the Middle East conflict. For non-specialists, the practical takeaway is that fuel-cost risk is now more policy-sensitive than it was a week earlier: temporary permissions can pull prices down fast, but the relief can fade just as quickly when the window closes or if logistics friction blocks deliveries.

What could overturn this: if shipping, insurance, or refinery constraints prevent those authorized barrels from moving through to usable product markets, the waiver’s real-world price effect could be much smaller than policy headlines suggest.
